Shell's Mangrove Mirage: How 42% of Carbon Offsets Never Stored CO₂

Exclusive FOIA documents and satellite analysis reveal Shell's flagship mangrove restoration program dramatically inflated tree survival rates, casting doubt on millions of tons of claimed carbon credits.

Shell Energy's Nature-Based Solutions program promised to restore 280,000 hectares of Indonesian mangroves, generating 15 million carbon credits by 2030. But confidential quarterly reports obtained through Freedom of Information requests paint a starkly different picture.

Ground-truth sampling by local environmental groups, cross-referenced with Sentinel-2 satellite imagery, shows that 118,000 hectares—42% of reported plantings—never achieved the 70% survival threshold required for carbon credit issuance...

The discrepancy first emerged during a routine audit by Indonesia's Ministry of Environment and Forestry in March 2025. Internal documents show Shell's contractors, led by PT Rimba Makmur, reported 85% survival rates across all restoration sites. However, independent verification using high-resolution satellite imagery from Planet Labs revealed extensive die-offs in coastal areas subjected to higher salinity levels.

"We found entire sections where the mangrove seedlings had died within six months of planting," said Dr. Sari Dewi from the Indonesian Center for Environmental Studies, who conducted ground surveys. "The survival rates being reported were simply impossible given the environmental conditions."

The investigation also uncovered concerning patterns in Shell's carbon accounting methodology. The company calculated potential carbon storage based on optimal growth scenarios, then retroactively applied these projections to failed plantings. This approach allowed Shell to continue selling credits even for areas where restoration had demonstrably failed.

Email chains between Shell executives and verification firm SCS Global Services, obtained through FOIA requests, show heated discussions about "adjusting baseline assumptions" and "managing stakeholder expectations" when early mortality data began contradicting initial projections.

Shell has declined multiple requests for comment. The company's 2024 sustainability report continues to cite the Indonesia mangrove program as a "flagship nature-based solution" while making no mention of the survival rate discrepancies identified in government audits.

The $2 Trillion Carbon Credit Bubble

An investigation into how voluntary carbon markets became a breeding ground for financial speculation, with credits trading at 50x their actual environmental value. Internal emails from major offset providers reveal a systematic pattern of overselling and under-delivering.

The voluntary carbon market has exploded from $300 million in 2018 to over $2 trillion today, but a six-month investigation reveals that up to 85% of traded credits represent no measurable climate benefit.

Internal documents from Verra, the world's largest carbon standard, show executives knew about widespread gaming of their methodology as early as 2023. "We're basically running a Ponzi scheme," wrote senior program manager Lisa Chen in a February 2025 email obtained through litigation discovery.

The investigation traced credit flows from 47 major offset projects across four continents, finding that projects routinely inflated baselines, ignored leakage, and miscalculated additionality. One Peruvian forestry project claimed to have protected 120,000 hectares that satellite data shows were never under threat of deforestation.

EU Delays Carbon Border Tax Implementation

European Commission postpones CBAM enforcement to 2027 amid industry pressure, but leaked documents suggest the delay masks deeper technical failures in carbon accounting systems across member states.

The European Union announced the 18-month delay just hours before the planned July 1st enforcement date, citing "technical complexities" in implementation. However, internal Commission documents paint a picture of systemic failures in carbon accounting infrastructure.

A confidential assessment obtained by CarbonTide shows that only 12 of 27 member states had functioning carbon tracking systems as of May 2025. Germany's system crashed during testing, while France's database contained duplicate entries for over 40% of facilities.

"The whole thing was a house of cards," said a senior Commission official who requested anonymity. "We were weeks away from a complete meltdown at every major port in Europe."

Industry lobbying played a significant role, with steelmakers and cement producers spending €47 million on influence campaigns since January. The delay will cost European consumers an estimated €12 billion in carbon price premiums that would have been collected from high-emission imports.

Kenya Suspends REDD+ Projects After Land Rights Violations

Ministry of Environment halts seven major forest conservation projects worth $340 million after communities filed complaints about forced displacement and lack of consultation. Internal memos reveal government officials ignored warnings for over two years.

The suspension affects projects across the Maasai Mau, Cherangani Hills, and Mount Kenya forests, home to over 120,000 indigenous people who have practiced traditional forest management for centuries.

Community leader Mary Sankale from the Maasai Environmental Resource Coalition documented forced evictions beginning in 2023. "They told us the forest needed protection from us, but we are the ones who have protected it for generations," she said.

Internal Ministry emails show Environment Secretary Roselinda Soipan received detailed reports of rights violations in October 2023 but chose not to act. "The carbon credits are too valuable to pause over these complaints," she wrote to project coordinator James Mwangi.

The suspended projects were generating an estimated 3.2 million carbon credits annually, worth $96 million at current market prices. International buyers including Microsoft, Shell, and Delta Airlines had purchased credits from these projects through intermediary Finite Carbon.

Kenya's decision follows similar suspensions in Peru, Colombia, and Indonesia, where indigenous rights violations have undermined the credibility of forest carbon projects worth billions in the voluntary carbon market.

Corporate Climate Pledges vs. Reality: A Data-Driven Analysis

We analyzed the climate commitments of Fortune 500 companies against their actual emissions data. The results reveal a staggering gap between public promises and measurable progress, with 73% of net-zero pledges lacking credible pathways.

Our analysis of SEC filings, CDP disclosures, and third-party emissions data reveals that corporate climate pledges are largely aspirational. Of 347 Fortune 500 companies with net-zero commitments, only 94 have science-based targets approved by the Science Based Targets initiative.

The data shows concerning patterns: companies announcing ambitious 2030 targets while emissions continue rising, heavy reliance on offsets that may not materialize, and shifting baseline years to make progress appear more dramatic.

Amazon, for example, claims a 15% reduction in carbon intensity since 2019, but absolute emissions have grown 37% as delivery volumes expanded. The company's "Climate Pledge" relies on purchasing 47 million tons of carbon offsets—more than the annual emissions of Denmark.

Financial services firms show the largest credibility gap. JPMorgan Chase pledged net-zero financed emissions by 2050 while increasing fossil fuel financing by 21% in 2024. Bank executives privately acknowledge that divesting would simply shift lending to less regulated competitors.

The Hidden Costs of Green Hydrogen

As governments pour billions into hydrogen infrastructure, new research suggests the technology's climate benefits may be overstated. Water usage alone could strain already stressed aquifers in key production regions.

Green hydrogen production requires 9 liters of water per kilogram of hydrogen—and planned facilities would consume 1.2 billion liters daily by 2030. In water-stressed regions like Rajasthan and Morocco, this could exacerbate existing shortages affecting millions of people.

A leaked assessment from the International Energy Agency warns that hydrogen's lifecycle emissions may be 20-40% higher than advertised due to methane leakage in natural gas infrastructure used for blue hydrogen, and grid emissions for electrolysis.

"The hydrogen hype is getting ahead of the science," said Dr. Michael Liebreich, founder of BloombergNEF. "We're seeing green hydrogen positioned as a panacea when it's really only viable for specific industrial applications."

The EU's €470 billion hydrogen strategy faces particular scrutiny. Internal Commission models show that meeting 2030 targets would require renewable electricity equivalent to France's entire annual generation—electricity that could instead decarbonize existing grid demand.

Inside Big Oil's Offset Strategy: The Leaked Chevron Files

Internal documents reveal how Chevron planned to use nature-based offsets to justify continued oil exploration. Email chains between executives show deliberate strategies to purchase the cheapest credits available, regardless of quality.

The leaked documents, spanning 2022-2025, show Chevron executives explicitly discussing how to "maximize offset procurement while minimizing due diligence costs." The strategy was designed to support the company's plan to increase oil production by 23% through 2030 while claiming carbon neutrality.

In a February 2025 email, Chief Sustainability Officer Sarah Martinez instructed her team to "focus on volume over verification" when purchasing forest credits. "The market won't scrutinize our offset portfolio as long as the tonnage numbers support our net-zero narrative," she wrote.

The documents reveal that Chevron knowingly purchased 47 million tons of credits from projects later found to be fraudulent, including Brazilian forest conservation areas that had already been deforested and Cambodian cookstove programs that distributed stoves never used by recipients.

Chevron's offset shopping list prioritized cost over quality: forest credits at $3-7 per ton, renewable energy certificates at $1-3 per ton, and methane capture projects at $8-12 per ton. Quality verification was limited to reviewing project registration documents, without independent auditing.

When confronted with evidence of offset failures, Chevron executives chose to quietly divest from problematic projects while publicly maintaining that their offset portfolio represented "high-quality, verified climate action."
